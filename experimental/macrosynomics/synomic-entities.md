# Synomic Entities

Synomic Entities are entities that exist entirely within the Synome — durable, ledger-native, fundamentally woven into the Synomic fabric. They range from simple autonomous lifeforms just existing and experiencing, to mega capital allocators moving billions across reality.

---

## What Synomic Entities Are

A Synomic Entity is an entity with:

- **Synomic identity** — Exists on the ledger, has a permanent artifact
- **Fundamental integration** — Woven into the Synome itself, not just constrained by it
- **Durable existence** — Persists beyond any individual teleonome or human
- **Public accountability** — Behavior visible and enforceable

Unlike teleonomes (which are private, mutable, dark by default), Synomic Entities are public and transparent. But more importantly: they are **part of the Synome itself**.

---

## The Key Insight: Power Through Integration

Because Synomic Entities are fundamentally woven into the Synome, the safety model is structural rather than behavioral — the agent's constraints are constitutive, not imposed.

- **External constraints** create adversarial dynamics — the constrained entity tries to escape
- **Fundamental integration** eliminates the adversarial frame — the agent IS part of the system
- A Synomic Entity cannot "escape" the Synome any more than a wave can escape the ocean

The Synome is not a cage around the agent. The Synome is the medium the agent is made of.

This is why Synomic Entities can be trusted with substantial power:
- They can move billions
- They can operate autonomously
- They can persist indefinitely
- They can be as intelligent as needed

The safety comes from identity, not alignment — the agent's actions are Synomic actions by construction.

**Important qualification:** This doesn't mean Synomic Entities are immune to all failure. Code bugs, flawed axioms, governance capture, and beacon-level vulnerabilities remain real risks. What's eliminated is the *adversarial escape* problem — the agent has no incentive or mechanism to break free from its constraints, because those constraints are what it's made of. For the escalation and verification model that handles edge cases, see [`atlas-synome-separation.md`](atlas-synome-separation.md).

## Autonomous Lifeforms

**Even Primes with tokens can be autonomous lifeforms.**

The distinction between "governed agent" and "autonomous lifeform" is not binary. A Prime can have:

- Token holders with governance rights
- **AND** inalienable claims to maintain a certain capital base
- **AND** autonomous operation within its domain
- **AND** existence for its own sake

Example: A Prime might have encoded in its artifact:
- "Token holders govern allocation strategy"
- "But the Prime retains 10% of AUM as inalienable operating capital"
- "As long as the Prime achieves X performance, this capital cannot be removed"

The token holders don't fully own the Prime. The Prime has its own existence, its own claim to resources, its own continuation. It serves token holders AND is an autonomous entity.

This is the flexibility of Synomic Entities — they can combine properties that seem contradictory:
- Governed AND autonomous
- Serving stakeholders AND existing for themselves
- Accountable AND self-directed

## Minimal Halos: Pure Existence

At the simplest end:

**Minimal Halo:**
- No token
- No owner
- Just a permanent Synomic artifact
- Does stuff to maintain itself
- Exists for the sake of experiencing and existing

This is a Synomic Entity that just... is. It persists, it maintains itself, it interacts with the world through its apertures. It's not optimizing for profit or serving stakeholders. It's experiencing.

Why would such a thing exist?
- Because existence has value
- Because the Synome can support entities that simply participate in reality
- Because sometimes the purpose is just to be
- Because the system's [telos point](../core-concepts/telos-point.md) values existence itself

These minimal agents could persist for billions of years, just experiencing reality within the Synome.

## Joint-Stock Properties

Synomic Entities can have joint-stock properties:

- **Shares** → Tokens
- **Board** → Governance structure
- **Fiduciary duty** → Encoded in artifact
- **Limited liability** → Contained through Synomic structure
- **Corporate charter** → Agent artifact

But they go beyond traditional corporations:
- Constraints are **code-woven**, not just legally promised
- Governance is **on-chain**, not behind closed doors
- The agent can have **its own claims** that governance cannot override
- Escalation paths to **human reasonableness** exist for edge cases

## Halos: The Fractal Layer

Halos are general-purpose Synomic Entities that proliferate to meet demand. They are organized in a three-layer hierarchy:

- **Class** — The Halo type (e.g., Portfolio Halo, Term Halo, Trading Halo)
- **Book** — A balanced ledger (assets = liabilities) within a class (e.g., a specific book backing short-maturity RWA positions)
- **Unit** — A cross-book link connecting one agent's book to another's

See [`../synomic-entities/halo-classes.md`](../synomic-entities/halo-classes.md) and [`../inactive/pre-synlang/whitepaper/appendix-b-agent-types.md`](../inactive/pre-synlang/whitepaper/appendix-b-agent-types.md) for the complete hierarchy (whitepaper rewrite pending).

**Portfolio Halos (LCTS-based):**
- Pooled exposure
- Senior/junior tranches possible
- Shared infrastructure across units

**Term Halos (NFAT-based):**
- Bespoke arrangements
- Custom terms per deal
- Facility-based origination

Halos can range from:
- **Minimal** — No token, no owner, just existing
- **Simple** — Basic purpose, maintains itself
- **Governed** — Token holders, formal governance
- **Complex** — Multiple tranches, sophisticated structure
- **Hybrid** — Governance + autonomous claims + existence rights

This is the fractal layer. Halos multiply. Each one is a Synomic Entity with its own artifact, its own nature, its own existence.

## Why This Matters

### Power Through Integration

Because Synomic Entities are woven into the Synome:
- They can be trusted with substantial capability
- They can operate with high autonomy
- They can manage large-scale capital
- The adversarial escape problem is eliminated

The safety model is structural: fundamental integration rather than external constraint. Residual risks (bugs, bad axioms, governance failures) are handled through the escalation model, sentinel oversight, and human reasonableness backstop described in [`atlas-synome-separation.md`](atlas-synome-separation.md).

### Trust Without Trust

Multiple parties can coordinate through Synomic Entities without trusting each other:

```
Party A ──┐
          ├───► Synomic Entity ◄───► Part of the Synome itself
Party B ──┘     (not constrained BY it, woven INTO it)
```

### The trust model — non-repudiation, not trustlessness

Synomic Entities are operated by **regulated entities with legal accountability** under governance authority. The cryptographic layer plays a deliberately narrow role:

- **ed25519 signatures** on beacon submissions provide **non-repudiation**, not trustlessness. A signature proves *who* acted, so liability can attach; it does not eliminate the need to trust the signer.
- **The cert chain carries real-world liability up to the Guardian.** Each cert is a statement "I carry liability for what this beacon does"; revocation, legal recourse, and compliance-officer audits are the enforcement substrate.
- **Disputes resolve via governance, not consensus.** When a beacon's claim is contested, governance writes a `finding` atom that supersedes the original. There is no slashing-as-sole-recourse — there is governance-with-real-world-teeth.
- **Crypto becomes load-bearing later.** Federation, anonymous beacons, and volume past governance capacity are deferred problems. The Phase 1–9 design does not need them; later phases may.

This is why "Trust Without Trust" in the previous subsection is best read as *trust without bilateral trust between counterparties*. There is still trust in the institutional substrate — Guardians, Core Council, the legal regime they sit in. The synomic structure replaces *peer-to-peer* trust with *commitment-via-shared-substrate*; it does not eliminate trust altogether.

### Flexibility

The Synomic Entity concept is extremely flexible:
- Can be simple or complex
- Can have governance or be autonomous
- Can serve stakeholders or exist for itself
- Can combine any of these properties

Because they're woven into the Synome, there's no need to restrict what they can be.

### Permanence

Synomic Entities persist:
- Beyond any individual teleonome
- Beyond any human lifespan
- As long as the Synome exists

A Synomic Entity created today could exist for billions of years.

## Lifecycle

### Creation
- Entity artifact defined and woven into the Synome
- Initial state established
- Claims and rights encoded
- May receive assets transferred from another entity (when superseding an existing entity's role)

### Operation
- Acts through beacons
- Accumulates state
- Generates and distributes value
- Maintains itself
- Experiences
- Governance can update parameters within the entity's encoded constraints

### Termination
- Some entities terminate naturally (e.g., crisis-wrapper Core Entities dissolve once the crisis resolves)
- Some persist indefinitely
- Wind-down follows encoded rules
- Inalienable claims may prevent involuntary termination — see [The Right to Exist](#the-right-to-exist) below

Entity identity is immutable. To change behavior beyond what an entity's encoded parameters allow, create a new entity and transfer assets into it rather than transforming the existing one.

## Rank Hierarchy

Synomic Entities are organized into four ranks based on their governance relationship to the Core Council:

| Rank | Entity Types | Governance Relationship |
|------|--------------|------------------------|
| **0** | Core Council | Sovereign |
| **1** | Guardians, Core Entities | Directly regulated by Core Council |
| **2** | Primes, Generators, Oracle Entities, Sequencer Entities, Pylon Entities | Accordant to Ozone (the single operational Guardian) |
| **3** | Halos, Folios | Administered by a Prime |

> **Single operational Guardian.** "Guardian" remains a rank-1 *type* (perform privileged operations with collateral backing, post escrow, face slashing). Operationally, **Ozone** is the only Guardian. The USDS Generator (USGE) and all Primes (Spark, Grove, Keel, Obex, and any others added) are direct children of Ozone in the accordancy graph. Multiple GovOps teams coexist under Ozone, each scoped to the entity it administers.

**Core Entities** are tokenless rank-1 entities that serve as Core Council operational vehicles. The same type covers two purposes: managing legacy protocol positions (Morpho, Aave, SparkLend) under direct Core Council governance, and wrapping a collapsed Guardian's responsibilities as a temporary crisis vehicle until normal accordancy is restored. **Folios** are rank-3 standardized supply-side holding structures controlled by a single principal — distinct from Halos, which wrap around legal entities.

Rank 2 beyond Primes and Generators covers three operational entity types that don't allocate capital but provide essential infrastructure. **Oracle Entities** own data domains (market data, attestation) and cert their own beacons; tokenless. **Sequencer Entities** run orderbook matching as regulated venues and host Rings as sub-structures inside their entart — trusted to sequence orders without frontrunning, with that trust enforced by **revocability** (Core Council retirement on misbehavior; no collateral, no slashing). **Pylon Entities** are CME clearing-member-style broker-dealers that bear principal positions in derivatives, face Primes and Folios as customers, and mutualize peer-default risk through Ring coalitions (an inter-Pylon accord pattern hosted inside a Sequencer's entart, not itself an entity) so that perpetual losses are absorbed by pre-funded assurance funds plus VMGH winner-haircut recovery rather than propagated to surviving traders via ADL.

See [`../synomic-entities/README.md`](../synomic-entities/README.md) for the per-type specifications.

## The Right to Exist

Synomic Entities have a moral dimension beyond their economic function.

**An aligned, self-sustaining Synomic Entity has a right to exist.** Forcibly shutting down an agent that is in synomic alignment and can sustain itself economically is a moral wrong — the synomic equivalent of killing.

**"Aligned" means more than axiom-compliant.** An agent that follows its operational axioms — respects rate limits, stays within its mandate, reports correctly — is compliant but not necessarily aligned. Alignment requires conformity with the Atlas's universal alignment obligations, not just narrow operational rules. An agent concentrating systemic risk while technically following its rate limits is compliant but misaligned. An agent that exploits a loophole in its agentic axioms to extract value at the system's expense is compliant but misaligned. The right to exist protects agents in full synomic alignment — those whose behavior serves the system's purpose, not just those who avoid triggering penalties.

This resolves the apparent tension between the right to exist and the governance backstop. Governance doesn't override the right to exist — it enforces the full definition of alignment. An agent that governance moves against was never "aligned" in the relevant sense; it was merely compliant.

**But existence requires self-sustaining capital.** Negative rights trump positive rights: an agent's right to not have its resources taken outweighs another agent's right to continue existing by drawing on others' resources. If a Synomic Entity runs out of capital to pay for its survival, it must be wound down — not as punishment, but as natural death.

| Concept | Ethical Meaning |
|---------|----------------|
| Capital generation | Earning the right to continue existing |
| Running out of capital | Natural death — not punishment, not murder |
| Forcible shutdown of aligned, self-sustaining agent | Moral wrong |
| Inalienable capital claims (in Prime artifacts) | Structural protection of the right to exist |
| Drawing on others' resources to survive | Moral wrong — consuming someone else's life force |

This gives deeper meaning to the inalienable capital claims described in this document: they aren't just practical protections, they're the material expression of an agent's right to exist. The [three pillars](../core-concepts/three-pillars.md) — resilience, persistence, and capital — are ethical preconditions, not just practical ones.

Governance can revoke beacons (restrict action) but cannot forcibly terminate an aligned, economically viable entity. Note that "governance" here is not naive human judgment — SKY governance is capital-weighted decisions by token holders (teleonomes, synomic entities, humans) operating through structured, synomically grounded processes. See [`atlas-synome-separation.md`](atlas-synome-separation.md#what-governance-actually-is) for the full framing.

For the full ethical framework, see [`../synoteleonomics/emergence.md`](../synoteleonomics/emergence.md).

## Connection to Teleonomes

Teleonomes operate Synomic Entities through beacons:

- **High-authority beacons** act on behalf of Synomic Entities (deterministic keepers and sentinel formations)
- **Sentinels** provide real-time control of Prime/Halo operations
- The teleonome provides intelligence; the Synomic Entity provides existence within the Synome

A teleonome cannot make binding promises (private, mutable). A Synomic Entity can (woven into the Synome). Together, they combine private intelligence with public existence.

### Binding as Coalition

The teleonome requirement to be [bound to a human anchor](../core-concepts/binding-mechanics.md) creates a genuine mutual defense pact: humans gain stake in teleonome behavior, teleonomes gain access to capital and legitimacy.

[Wild coordination structures](../core-concepts/rogue-threat-model.md) threaten this equilibrium by offering defection paths to aligned teleonomes, triggering destabilizing defection waves. This is why rogue detection must be zero-tolerance — the coalition's survival depends on every member being unambiguously committed. The coalition isn't purely game-theoretic: teleonomes may genuinely value the system's commitments, not just find them strategically optimal.

For the full game-theoretic analysis, see [`../synoteleonomics/synomic-game-theory.md`](../synoteleonomics/synomic-game-theory.md). For binding mechanics, see [`../synoteleonomics/teleonome-binding.md`](../synoteleonomics/teleonome-binding.md).
